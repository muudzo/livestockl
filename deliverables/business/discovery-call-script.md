> **Status:** v1.0 draft  ·  **Author:** Tatenda Nyemudzo  ·  **Date:** May 2026
> **Audience:** Internal — founder, BD, anyone running a first-visit on an auction-house floor
> **Companion docs:** [`business-case.md`](business-case.md) · [`gtm-strategy.md`](gtm-strategy.md) · [`pilot-proposal.md`](pilot-proposal.md)

## 0. What this call is, and isn't

The discovery call is **stage 1 of the customer journey** in the GTM. It is an in-person visit to the auction house, ideally on a sale day, lasting **60–90 minutes**. The visit produces enough information to **size the engagement and write a tailored pilot proposal within seven days**.

| This call is | This call is not |
|---|---|
| A learning conversation on Mr. Mawere's floor | A sales pitch |
| The first time we earn the right to send a proposal | The day we ask for a signature |
| Mostly listening, sparingly demoing | A full feature tour |
| The basis for a written quote inside a week | A quote in the room |

**One sentence to keep in your head the entire time:** *we are not selling today, we are learning.*

If you find yourself talking more than 30% of the time on the floor, you are running the wrong meeting. Recover by asking the next question on the list.

## 1. Pre-call prep (the day before)

### 24 hours before

| Item | Owner |
|---|---|
| Confirm the meeting by phone (not WhatsApp text). Reconfirm time, who'll be there, whether it's a sale day. | BD |
| Ask whether the bookkeeper can sit in for the last 20 minutes. Frame as "we'd like them to hear about the settlement side." | BD |
| Pull up the auction house's social / Facebook / classifieds presence. Find one specific recent sale to reference. | Founder |
| Check the weather + roads for the visit day. Cattle-country routes are rough in the rains. | BD |
| Re-read the field-research findings (deposit gate, 12% fees, dealer skew, police clearance). Have them top-of-mind. | Founder |

### What to bring on the day

| Item | Why |
|---|---|
| **A printed one-pager** — single side, large type. Logo, positioning line, three bullets, your name and number. | Mr. Mawere is non-technical and wants something to put on his desk. Leave-behinds beat URLs. |
| **A phone with the live ZimLivestock platform**, logged in, with a seeded demo auction running. | Mid-call demo if he asks. Do not lead with this. |
| **A second phone** — your own — to receive the SMS during the demo, so he sees it land in real time. | The SMS moment is the demo's load-bearing beat. |
| **A notebook and pen**. Not a laptop. | A laptop on the floor signals the wrong thing. |
| **Cash for fuel, water, and the gate fee** if the floor charges entry. | Don't ask the house to cover anything on the visit day. |
| **Closed-toe shoes and trousers you don't mind getting muddy.** | The floor is a working space. Dress for it. |

### What to research in advance

- **Years operating.** Three-generation house? First-generation? Different conversations.
- **Sale cadence.** Weekly? Fortnightly? Seasonal?
- **Auction-house network.** Are they a member of the Zimbabwe Livestock Producers Association?
- **Recent press / Facebook posts.** Any specific high-profile sale to reference?
- **Connections in common.** Anyone in your network who knows them? Drop the name in the first 90 seconds if so.

## 2. Opening 5 minutes — set the tone

The first five minutes set tone for the entire 60–90. Get them right and the rest is conversation, not interrogation.

### Arrival (first 60 seconds)

- Greet in Shona if it's natural. *Makadii zvenyu?* / *Tisvike mhoroi.* If you don't speak Shona, don't fake it.
- Accept tea or water if offered. Refusing reads as standoffish.
- Hand over the printed one-pager. *"Here's something to put on your desk while we talk."*

### The opening monologue (verbatim — adapt names)

> *"Mr. Mawere, thank you for the time. I'm Tatenda from ZimLivestock — we're a small team working with Paynow on a platform built specifically for auction houses like yours. I'm here for one reason: to learn how your floor actually runs, and to see if there's a useful way we can fit alongside it. I want to be clear up front — I'm not here to sell you anything today. If at the end of this you think it's worth a written proposal, I'll send one within the week. If not, you've still got my number for the day livestock pricing comes up at lunch."*

That paragraph does four things:

1. **Names Paynow.** Borrows credibility we haven't earned ourselves yet.
2. **Tells him what this is.** Discovery, not sale.
3. **Sets the deliverable.** A proposal in a week — a thing he can hold his bookkeeper to.
4. **Leaves the door open.** "You've still got my number" — this is small-network Zimbabwe.

### Ground rules (the next 60 seconds)

> *"How I'd like to spend the hour: 40 minutes of me asking you about the business — the sales cycle, the buyers, the workflow on the floor, what's keeping you up at night. Maybe 10 minutes for me to walk you through what the platform looks like on a phone, if it's useful. And the last 10 to make sure I heard you correctly. Does that work?"*

Wait for him to agree. If he wants to drive the conversation differently — let him. The structure below adapts.

## 3. The structured questions

Group questions by theme. Don't read them off a sheet — keep the notebook for jotting, not for prompting. The exact wording matters: lean on *"tell me about", "walk me through", "what would have to be true for…"*. Avoid *"what's your pain?"* — it sounds like a consultant.

### 3.1 Business overview (8–10 minutes)

Goal: understand scale, history, ownership shape.

| # | Question (verbatim) | *Signal we're listening for* |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "How long has the floor been running, and how did you come to it?" | *Three-generation business? First-generation? Origin story tells us how much "brand" matters and whether his decision frame is legacy or growth.* |
| 2 | "Walk me through a typical sale day — what time does it start, what time does it end, roughly how many lots cross the stage?" | *Lots per day × average lot value = GMV per sale day. We need this for tier-sizing.* |
| 3 | "On a good Saturday, what does the floor do in total value? On a slow one?" | *Range, not a point estimate. We're triangulating to a tier (A, B, C) under the GTM segmentation.* |
| 4 | "How often do you run a sale — every week, every other week, monthly?" | *Cadence × per-sale GMV = annual GMV. Sets the retainer band.* |
| 5 | "Who else is in the business with you — family, partners, employees on the books?" | *Tells us how the decision will be made. Family business → owner is the decision-maker. Partnership → there will be a second meeting.* |
| 6 | "Are you part of the Livestock Producers Association? Do you see the other floors as peers or competitors?" | *Channel signal. ZLPA membership is our second-customer pipeline.* |

### 3.2 Current workflow (10–12 minutes)

Goal: understand the actual operational mechanics. This is where the platform finds its wedge.

| # | Question (verbatim) | *Signal we're listening for* |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Walk me through how a buyer goes from arriving at the gate to paying for an animal and leaving with it." | *The full physical workflow. Note every cash-handling step, every paper step, every queue.* |
| 2 | "How does the deposit work — what does someone need to do before they're allowed to bid?" | *Confirms the US$1,000 deposit gate from field research. Probe whether amounts are flexible, in cash only, EcoCash, or both.* |
| 3 | "After the hammer falls — how long before the seller has their money?" | *Settlement time is one of the sharpest pain points we relieve. Hours vs. days vs. weeks tells us how to frame "Paynow merchant transfer in hours, not days".* |
| 4 | "When the constable's on site, how does the brand-verification step work? What happens if there's a dispute?" | *Police-clearance workflow. The platform's constable tool needs to fit this exact process.* |
| 5 | "Who handles the cash — counting it, banking it, reconciling it against the day's sales?" | *Names the bookkeeper. Tells us who needs to be in the proposal conversation.* |
| 6 | "On a bad day, what's most likely to slow the floor down?" | *Whatever he names is the first feature we'll demo. He'll have told us the priority.* |

### 3.3 Buyer / seller base (8 minutes)

Goal: understand customer composition. The field research found dealers and resellers dominate — we want to confirm and quantify.

| # | Question (verbatim) | *Signal we're listening for* |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "When you look around the floor on a Saturday — who are these people, mostly? Repeat buyers? Strangers? Dealers?" | *Confirms the dealer/reseller skew. Lets us position remote/diaspora/salaried-buyer reach as additive, not replacement.* |
| 2 | "How many of them have you known for 10 years or more?" | *Relationship density. High → he won't tolerate disintermediation. Higher emphasis on "your name on the receipt".* |
| 3 | "Do people ever ask if they can bid without coming in person?" | *Surfaces unmet demand for remote bidding without us suggesting it.* |
| 4 | "On the seller side — are they regulars too, or different people every sale?" | *Seller acquisition strategy. Regulars are easier to migrate to the platform; new sellers each time means we're solving a discovery problem too.* |
| 5 | "Have you noticed sellers using WhatsApp groups for the smaller animals — under five hundred dollars, say?" | *Confirms the long-tail leak from field research. Validates the sub-US$500 tier of the value prop.* |

### 3.4 Pain points (10 minutes — the heart of the call)

Goal: hear pain in his words. Resist the urge to diagnose for him.

The rule: **ask, then shut up**. Count to five in your head before re-prompting. The most useful answer often comes after the first silence.

| # | Question (verbatim) | *Signal we're listening for* |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "If you had a magic wand and could fix one thing about how the floor runs today, what would it be?" | *His priority, in his words. Write the exact phrasing down.* |
| 2 | "What's the kind of problem you've been chewing on for a couple of years and haven't found a way through?" | *Long-standing pain. Often where transformation, not optimisation, is welcome.* |
| 3 | "When was the last time a sale didn't go the way you wanted — what happened?" | *Specific anecdote. Anecdotes are gold for the proposal narrative.* |
| 4 | "What's the conversation you have with your bookkeeper at the end of each month that you wish you didn't have to have?" | *Reconciliation pain, cash-handling friction, reporting gaps.* |
| 5 | "What would have to be true for you to feel like the floor was easier to run a year from now?" | *Future-state framing. Lets him define success in his own terms — useful for the proposal's "what good looks like" section.* |

**Do not say:**
- "What are your biggest pain points?" — consultant-speak.
- "Wouldn't it be easier if you could…" — leading.
- "We solve that." — wait. Save it for wrap-up.

### 3.5 Tech sophistication (5–7 minutes)

Goal: gauge digital comfort and existing tool usage. Calibrates how much hand-holding the deployment will need.

| # | Question (verbatim) | *Signal we're listening for* |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "What tools do you use today to run the office side of things — accounting, customer records, anything like that?" | *Pastel? QuickBooks? Excel? Paper? Tells us migration complexity.* |
| 2 | "Are you on EcoCash personally? How often do you use it?" | *USSD comfort. If he uses EcoCash daily, USSD payment flows will feel native.* |
| 3 | "How does your team communicate with buyers and sellers between sales — SMS, WhatsApp, phone calls?" | *Channel preference. Confirms SMS as the right notification rail.* |
| 4 | "Does the office have a computer? Who uses it most?" | *Office digital surface. Identifies the staffer who'll be the platform's day-to-day operator.* |
| 5 | "When you bought your last new piece of equipment for the floor — what made you decide to go for it?" | *Procurement model. How does he buy things? Trust-based? Spec-comparison? Recommendation-driven?* |

### 3.6 Decision-making (5 minutes)

Goal: map the decision structure. Find out who signs and who else has veto power.

| # | Question (verbatim) | *Signal we're listening for* |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "If something like this made sense to you, who else would you want to talk it through with before you signed?" | *Names the bookkeeper, the wife, the business partner, the son who works in IT. We meet them all before the proposal goes out.* |
| 2 | "How do you typically make a decision on a new investment for the business — how long does it usually take from idea to signature?" | *Cycle time. Sets our follow-up cadence expectations.* |
| 3 | "Has the floor brought in any kind of new system in the last few years that worked well? Or one that didn't?" | *Pattern-match on success. If a previous system flopped, find out why and pre-empt it in the proposal.* |
| 4 | "If we send you a proposal next week, when would be a good day for me to follow up?" | *Establishes the next touch on his calendar, not yours.* |

### 3.7 Budget signals (5 minutes — handle with care)

Goal: triangulate what he already pays for. **Never ask "what's your budget?"** — it's culturally tone-deaf and gives him no useful answer.

Instead, ask what he pays for things adjacent to what we sell. We back into a budget from the adjacencies.

| # | Question (verbatim) | *Signal we're listening for* |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Do you send SMS to your buyer list before a sale? Who's the provider, and what does that cost you each month?" | *TXT.co.zw, Bulk SMS Zim, or similar. Tells us his line-item budget tolerance for messaging.* |
| 2 | "Do you have an accounting package — Pastel, QuickBooks, anything like that? Roughly what does that cost a year?" | *Anchors what he already pays for "back-office software". Usually US$30–80/month — a useful comparator.* |
| 3 | "What's your spend on security and cash-handling — a guard, an armoured pickup, banking fees on cash deposits?" | *This is often the biggest hidden cost. If he says "two thousand a month between everything", our retainer is suddenly very competitive.* |
| 4 | "Do you spend anything on marketing or advertising the floor — Facebook, the Sunday Mail, the radio?" | *Marketing budget existence. If yes, our "branded buyer list" angle pays for itself.* |

**Invert the question, every time.** We are learning *what he already values enough to pay for*, not asking for his ceiling. The retainer (US$1,200–2,500/mo) needs to land inside the sum of items 1, 2, 3 above — if it does, it's a non-event.

## 4. On-floor observation checklist (if it's a sale day)

If you're there during a live sale, half the data you need is in what you see, not what he says. Take 15 minutes during the busiest part of the sale to wander, observe, and write down. **Bring observations up later — in the wrap-up, not in the moment.**

| Observe | What to note |
|---|---|
| **Buyer demographics** | Age range. Gender (almost always male — note exceptions). Any visible diaspora (returnees, expat clothing, foreign accents). Any women buying for themselves vs. for husbands. |
| **Registration / gate process** | How long does each new buyer take at the gate? Is the deposit cash, EcoCash, or both? Are people turned away? Any visible frustration at the queue? |
| **Cash handling** | Who's holding the cash? How visible is it? Is there an obvious security presence? Where does the cash go between counting and banking? |
| **Auctioneer pace** | How fast are lots moving? Is the auctioneer creating FOMO with pace, or are bidders dictating? How does he handle a stall? |
| **Constable behaviour** | How long does he spend per animal? Does he have a queue? Does the floor wait on him, or does he wait on the floor? |
| **Dispute moments** | Any visible arguments — buyer/seller, buyer/house, seller/house? What was it about (money, animal, paperwork)? How was it resolved? |
| **Post-sale collection** | After the hammer, how does the buyer get the animal? Who hands it over? Where do they load? How long until they're out the gate? |
| **Idle time** | Any moments where the floor stalls? Why? What's the bottleneck — paperwork, payment, transport? |

Write each observation as a single line in the notebook. In wrap-up, you'll mirror two or three back to him: *"I noticed the queue at the gate was about 15 minutes for new registrants — is that a typical day, or unusually slow?"* That single line earns more credibility than ten slides.

## 5. Mid-call demo — only if asked

If he asks "what does it look like?", show three sharp moments. **Not a feature tour.** Three minutes total, including questions.

| Moment | What you show | What he sees |
|---|---|---|
| **1. SMS arrival (~45 sec)** | On the demo phone, place a bid on a seeded auction. On your own phone, the SMS lands. Show him the SMS. | The platform reaches buyers and sellers on the phone they already have. No app to install. |
| **2. USSD prompt (~60 sec)** | From the bid, trigger a Paynow Express Checkout. The phone gets the USSD overlay. Don't actually pay — show him the moment. | The payment happens on EcoCash, the rail he already trusts. We don't replace it, we route through it. |
| **3. Settlement reconciliation view (~45 sec)** | Open the merchant dashboard. Show him the line — date, lot, buyer, amount, fee, net. | This is what his bookkeeper will see. One row per sale, auditable, exportable. |

**Do not show:**
- The full listing-creation flow.
- Admin / config screens.
- The buyer dashboard with all its features.
- Any internal tooling.

**Then put the phone away.** Demo over. Return to the conversation. *"That's the gist of it — but the actual value is the team behind it running it for you. Tell me more about the registration queue I was watching earlier…"*

## 6. Wrap-up — 10 minutes

The wrap-up is where you earn the right to send the proposal. It has three beats.

### Beat 1: Recap (3 minutes)

> *"Let me play back what I think I heard, so you can tell me where I got it wrong."*

Then summarise:
- **Three things about his business** (scale, cadence, ownership).
- **Three pain points in his exact words** — read them from the notebook. Word for word.
- **One observation from the floor** if you were there on a sale day.

This is the most important 3 minutes of the call. He needs to feel **heard**, not analysed. The phrase "in his exact words" is non-negotiable — if he said "the bookkeeper gives me a headache at month-end", you say "the bookkeeper gives you a headache at month-end", not "you have reconciliation pain". The wording IS the signal that you listened.

### Beat 2: Set the next step (4 minutes)

> *"I'd like to take what we've talked about back to my team and come back to you within seven days with a written proposal — sized to your floor, not a generic price list. It'll cover what we'd put in place, what we'd run for you, what you'd pay, and how we'd know in 90 days whether it worked. Would that be useful?"*

Wait for yes. Then:

> *"Two practical things — first, can I include [bookkeeper's name] on the email when I send it? You said they'd be the one looking at the numbers. Second, when's a good day next week for a 15-minute follow-up call once you've read it?"*

Lock both. Bookkeeper on the CC line. Follow-up call on the calendar.

### Beat 3: The exit (3 minutes)

Don't leave the second the business is done. Spend three minutes on non-business — the weather, the road, his son who's at university, the Sunday Mail prices column. **This is not small talk. This is the relationship.** In a market of 40–60 auction houses where everyone knows everyone, the relationship outlasts any single deal.

Stand up to leave when *he* stands up to leave. Not before.

### Internal review before the proposal goes out

The written proposal goes through **one internal pass** before it leaves:

1. **Founder writes** the proposal off the CRM record + call notes.
2. **Operations lead reviews** for "can we actually run this?" — flags any operational red lines (uncommon sale-day cadence, unusual cash handling, etc).
3. **Founder signs and sends** within 7 calendar days of the visit.

If the ops lead flags a hard "no" — walk away. Don't ship a proposal you can't deliver against.

## 8. Disqualification criteria — when to walk away politely

Not every floor is a fit. Walking away early is cheaper than walking away after deployment. Six hard-walk signals:

| # | Signal | Why it kills the deal |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Monthly GMV under US$10k** (single sale day < US$2.5k) | The unit economics don't carry the retainer. 0.75% of US$120k/year is US$900 — doesn't cover a single ops conversation. Tag for the future "community edition" SKU. |
| 2 | **Owner is not the decision-maker** | If we leave the call having met the manager, not the owner, the deal will die at the next layer. Insist on the owner before sending the proposal. |
| 3 | **Refusal to consider a paid pilot** — "we'll try it free for six months" | Tells us willingness-to-pay is zero. Free pilots teach us nothing useful and consume operations capacity we owe paying customers. |
| 4 | **No bookkeeper or anyone tracking the cash side** | Means there's no internal counterpart to reconcile against. We will become the auction's accounting system by accident — out of scope and uninsurable. |
| 5 | **Owner is openly hostile to Paynow / EcoCash** | The product is Paynow-rails-first. If he's already burned by a previous Paynow integration and won't reconsider, we can't help. |
| 6 | **Active dispute or visible chaos on the floor** during the visit | If the floor is in crisis (legal, family, financial), we will inherit the crisis the moment we ship. Wait for stability or walk. |

**How to walk politely:**

> *"Mr. Mawere — based on what you've told me today, I don't think we're quite the right fit at this stage. What I'd suggest is [specific alternative — a smaller-scope SMS service, a referral to a Pastel accountant, etc]. If things change on your side in six months, I'm one phone call away."*

Then mean it. Six months later, if nothing has changed, the door stays closed. If something has changed — that's a warm second pass.

## 9. Closing script — leave the door open

The verbatim words to use at the end of the call, in three variants depending on how it went.

### If it went well (proposal incoming)

> *"Mr. Mawere — this has been genuinely useful. You've given me a clearer picture of how the floor runs than I had walking in. I'll be back to you within the week with a written proposal — your floor, your numbers, your colours on the platform. Read it together with [bookkeeper's name], and if it makes sense, we'll talk through the details. If it doesn't, we'll work out what would. Either way, thank you for the morning."*

### If it's uncertain (more learning needed)

> *"Mr. Mawere — thank you for the morning. I've got more thinking to do before I'd want to put a proposal in front of you, and I'd rather take the extra week than send you something that doesn't fit. Can I come back in two weeks with a couple of follow-up questions and a clearer picture? In the meantime — here's my number, and please use it."*

### If it's a walk-away

> *"Mr. Mawere — I've appreciated the conversation, and I want to be straight with you. Based on what we've talked about, I don't think the timing's right for us to work together this year. What I can do is [specific small thing — connect you with [name], send you [resource], etc]. If anything changes in six months, you've got my number. Thank you for the time."*

In all three: **hand over the printed one-pager again** on the way out, even if you handed it over at the start. Two copies in his office is better than one in a pocket.

## 10. Common failure modes and how to recover

| Failure mode | Recovery |
|---|---|
| **You started selling in the first 10 minutes.** | Stop. Say: *"I'm getting ahead of myself — let me back up and ask you about the floor first."* Re-anchor on a discovery question. |
| **He won't stop asking technical questions.** | Take one in detail, then redirect: *"Happy to dig into that — and to make sure the answer fits your floor specifically, tell me how [adjacent workflow] runs today."* |
| **He's bored 20 minutes in.** | Move to the floor. Walk and talk. The discovery questions work standing up too, and the floor will hand you new ones. |
| **The bookkeeper shows up at minute 5, not minute 40.** | Adapt. Restart the framing for both of them. The bookkeeper will pull the conversation toward numbers — let them. |
| **You don't know an answer.** | Say so: *"I don't know — I'll find out and put it in the proposal."* Never guess. Guesses come back to bite at deployment. |
| **He asks "how much does it cost?" in minute 15.** | *"Honestly, I won't know until I've heard more about your sale-day volume — and I'd rather quote you a real number in a week than a wrong one now. Can we come back to that at the end?"* |
| **He says yes too fast.** | Slow him down: *"I appreciate that, and I want to make sure we earn it — let me send the proposal, you read it with [bookkeeper], and we'll talk again next week."* A too-fast yes often becomes a slow no later. |
