# Peer-Presentation Speaker Script

**Companion to:** [`internship-return-day.pptx`](internship-return-day.pptx)
**Audience:** Year-2 CMD students at NHL Stenden — about to look for their internships in June.
**Length:** 5–7 minutes spoken; 10 slides.
**Tone:** peer-to-peer, honest, slightly self-deprecating. Not a sales pitch.

---

## Opening note to yourself before you walk on

You're talking to the version of you from nine months ago. They're not impressed by titles, they're not impressed by Paynow's logo, and they're sceptical of anyone saying "do what I did." What they *are* hungry for is **the specific stuff their tutors won't tell them** — the application playbook, the choice tradeoffs, the unglamorous bits. Pitch the whole talk at that.

If you only remember one thing on stage: **the four "wish I'd known" slides are the only part that matters to the audience.** Slides 1–5 are setup so they trust you when you get to those. Don't over-rehearse the setup; over-rehearse 6–9.

---

## Slide 1 — Title  ·  ~10 seconds

> *Walk on, look up, pause.*

**Spoken:**
> "Hey. I spent the last nine weeks back home in Zimbabwe, building a marketplace for cattle. I'm going to tell you what it's like, why I picked it, and four things I wish someone had told me when I was sitting where you're sitting right now."

**Don't say:** "Hi my name is Tatenda" — wait. The slide says your name. Get to the hook.

---

## Slide 2 — Who I am  ·  ~30 seconds

**Spoken:**
> "I'm Tatenda. I'm in your year — CMD year three. I'm Zimbabwean, which is the relevant fact for this talk. I went **back** to Zimbabwe for my internship, which is the *less* relevant fact for this talk, because the lessons I'm about to share aren't about going abroad — they're about how you scope an internship.
>
> Most of you start looking in June. Some of you already know where you want to go. The rest of you are going to scroll stagecmd@nhlstenden.com for two weeks and panic. I did some version of that too. So I want to use my seven minutes to give you the four pieces of advice that would've saved me a lot of that panic."

**Don't dwell.** Move on as soon as the three columns have landed.

---

## Slide 3 — What Paynow does  ·  ~45 seconds

**Spoken:**
> "Quick context — because most of you have never heard of Paynow. Think of it as Stripe, except built for a country where most people don't have a credit card. Paynow processes the majority of online payments in Zimbabwe. They have over a hundred billers in their catalog — that's how you pay your electricity, your school fees, your council rates. The whole rail runs on mobile money via USSD prompts on feature phones.
>
> Why does this matter for the talk? Because Paynow was the opposite of what my CMD coursework had trained me to design for. I had to **unlearn** a lot of European-startup assumptions before I could design anything that actually worked here. We'll come back to that."

**Pacing tip:** the three stats on screen — let the audience read them, don't read them out loud.

---

## Slide 4 — What I'm doing  ·  ~55 seconds

**Spoken:**
> "What I built was something called ZimLivestock. It's a marketplace for cattle, goats, and sheep — running on every product in Paynow's stack.
>
> But before I tell you what it does, I want to tell you *why* it exists. Because early in the internship, the Paynow MD told me something that completely reframed the project for me — "

> *click — the MD quote highlights*

> "He said, 'Don't build a thesis. Build software you can sell.' And so I picked a project I could actually *sell* — a piece of software you could deploy at a Zimbabwean auction house, so they could run their physical floor as a digital marketplace. That commercial framing changed everything about how I approached the work.
>
> Two outputs ran in parallel from there. **First** — the production app. A real one. Real users, real Paynow integration, real money — tiny amounts, but real. **Second** — a 42-page benchmark of Paynow's developer experience compared to Stripe, Paystack, Flutterwave, two others. First-attempt evidence — I integrated each one as if I were a new developer, and wrote down everything that got in my way.
>
> That benchmark, by the way, was the first external one Paynow had ever received."

**Don't oversell the benchmark.** The audience can read "42-page" on screen — just land it. The MD quote is the load-bearing line on this slide. Let it breathe before you move on.

---

## Slide 5 — How I applied  ·  ~60 seconds

This is the slide they came to hear. Slow down.

**Spoken:**
> "Right — applications. Here's the bit I think most of you actually came for.
>
> I didn't get this internship through a job board. Paynow wasn't on stagecmd@nhlstenden.com. They weren't recruiting interns at all when I emailed them. So three things had to be true for this to work."

> *click — number 01*

> "**One — I picked the company first, the role second.** I knew I wanted to work on Zimbabwean fintech specifically. I made a list of companies that did that work, and *then* I asked what role might exist there. Most students do this backwards — they pick a role they think they want, then find any company that has it. That's how you end up making coffee at a Dutch agency that didn't need an intern."

> *click — number 02*

> "**Two — I cold-emailed the right person, not careers@.** I went on LinkedIn, found one specific engineer at Paynow who looked like he'd care about my work, and I sent him **one** email pitching a **specific** project I'd build for them if they'd have me. Not 'I want to learn.' Not 'I want to gain experience.' A specific project. Their reply came in two days."

> *click — number 03*

> "**Three — I followed up.** Twice. Most students stop after the first email. Most companies miss the first email. The follow-up is where the conversation actually starts. If anyone here is going to take *one* tactical thing from this talk, it's: write the follow-up before you write the first email, so you're not scared to send it."

> *pause for effect*

---

## Slide 6 — Wish I'd known #01  ·  ~45 seconds

**Spoken:**
> "Four things I wish someone had told me. First one —
>
> **Pick where you can ship, not where you can observe.** A small or distant company will hand you the keys on day one. A big-brand agency will ask you to assist. For your CMD portfolio — for the report you have to write at the end — *ownership beats prestige*. Every single time. The work you do is the only thing the report can be about. Pick a place that lets you actually do some."

**Personal anecdote you can drop in if time:**
> "I was the only intern at Paynow. There was no template for what I should be doing. That sounded terrifying on day one and turned out to be the best thing about the job."

---

## Slide 7 — Wish I'd known #02  ·  ~45 seconds

**Spoken:**
> "Second —
>
> **If the brief is ambiguous, that's the gift.** My brief on day one was three lines long. I panicked. I asked for more detail. Nobody gave me more detail. By week three I realised the open-endedness was the most valuable thing my supervisor could have given me — every scoping decision was mine to make, every piece of work was mine to own.
>
> If your supervisor on day one says 'we trust you to figure out what to build' — that is *not* a red flag. That is the green light. Get good at scoping."

---

## Slide 8 — Wish I'd known #03  ·  ~40 seconds

**Spoken:**
> "Third — and this one's specifically for CMD —
>
> **Your competences are broader than 'design'.** I shipped UX flows. I also shipped system architecture, a 42-page research report, four payment integrations, a live demo to company leadership. Every one of those mapped to an HBO-i competence. We get trained to think CMD means 'visual design' — but the framework is broader than that.
>
> Don't pre-narrow what you'll do. The internship that hires you for 'UX design' will let you do a lot more than UX design if you ask."

---

## Slide 9 — Wish I'd known #04  ·  ~40 seconds

**Spoken:**
> "Fourth — and this is the boring one, but please listen —
>
> **Start writing the report in week one.** Every tutor tells you this. Everybody ignores it. Don't. Half my deliverables exist *because* I wrote them down weekly — not because I sat down at the end and tried to remember what happened.
>
> Treat the report as a journal you write *towards*, not a deliverable you write *at the end*. A weekly README per phase, photos of every meeting, a paragraph after every demo. By the time the final report is due, you're editing. You're not authoring from scratch on a deadline. That's the difference between a calm last week and a miserable one."

---

## Slide 10 — Close  ·  ~30 seconds

**Spoken (slower, lower):**
> "Last thing.
>
> Internships are leverage. Nine weeks of full-time, focused work, with people who actually care about what you make, is worth more than a year of coursework. But the lever only multiplies what you put on it. Pick where you can *actually pull on the lever* — and that almost never means picking the most familiar option.
>
> I'll be around afterwards. Happy to talk about cold-emailing fintech companies, what an internship in Zimbabwe actually costs, how the CMD framework holds up when most of your work is code, or anything else.
>
> Thanks."

> *Step back. Don't say 'any questions'. Wait for the room.*

## Quick Q&A prep — three questions you'll almost certainly get

### "Did you get paid?"
Answer honestly. Internship pay in Zimbabwe is different from the Netherlands; quote a real number if you're comfortable, or say "less than a Dutch agency would have paid me, but I was living with family, so the trade was reasonable." Don't dodge — they want to know.

### "How much help did you get from AI / Claude?"
You've thought hard about this one already. Short version of the answer: *"AI accelerated the code, but the scoping, the research methodology, the demos, the writing — that was me. The benchmark Paynow saw was my analysis. The advice I'm giving you in this talk is mine. The seventy-page integration writeup is also mine, even though Claude helped me draft sections of it. The distinction that matters is: who decided what to do? That was always me."*

### "Would you do an internship abroad if you weren't already from the country?"
Be honest. Going back home was a special case. For most of them, an internship within an hour of campus is the right call. Don't oversell the foreign-internship thing — they didn't come to hear you brag about flying somewhere.

## Rehearsal notes

- **Time the whole thing once, end to end.** Aim for 6:00. If you hit 7:30 you need to cut.
- **First place to cut:** slide 3 (Paynow primer). You can shorten it to 25 seconds.
- **Last place to cut:** the four "wish I'd known" slides. Those are the whole talk.
- **Make eye contact during slides 6–9 specifically.** That's where you want the room with you.
- **Don't read the slides.** Anything on screen, *they* read. You add the human commentary.

Good luck.
