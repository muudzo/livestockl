# Livestock Auction Field Research — 19 March 2026

> First-hand observations from attending a physical livestock auction in Zimbabwe.
> Purpose: Understand the incumbent experience so ZimLivestock can compete.

---

## 1. Barrier to Entry: US$1,000 Bidding Deposit

Every potential buyer must pay a **US$1,000 deposit** before they can place a single bid. No deposit, no paddle.

**What it achieves:** Filters out non-serious participants and guarantees sellers that winning bids are backed by cash.

**The problem:** It prices out smallholder farmers, first-time buyers, and anyone who wants to buy a single goat for US$80. A thousand dollars is real money in a country where average monthly income sits around US$200-300.

**Our opportunity:** Smaller, refundable escrow deposits (US$50-100 held via Paynow) would maintain seriousness while dropping the barrier by ~90%. Release the hold if the buyer doesn't win.

---

## 2. Fee Structure: 12% Total House Take

| Party | Fee | On a US$1,200 sale |
|-------|-----|---------------------|
| Seller | 5% | -US$60 |
| Buyer | 7% | +US$84 |
| **House total** | **12%** | **US$144** |

After fees, transport, and police clearance costs, the net for both buyer and seller is significantly less than the headline price.

**Our opportunity:** A **5-6% total fee** (2% seller, 3-4% buyer) — roughly half the auction house — while still being sustainable. Going lower than 3% total risks looking unsustainable and won't cover dispute resolution, support, and infrastructure.

---

## 3. Auction Prices vs. Facebook/WhatsApp Prices

Auction prices are **consistently higher** than informal Facebook Marketplace and WhatsApp group prices for comparable animals.

**Why:** The auction house provides trust infrastructure — verified animals, police clearance, guaranteed payment, physical inspection. Facebook is cheaper because it's riskier (no escrow, no verification, high no-show rate).

**Key insight:** We shouldn't try to match Facebook prices. We should offer **auction-level trust at lower fees**. The value proposition isn't "cheapest cattle" — it's "trustworthy transactions without the 12% cut."

---

## 4. The Auctioneer Problem: Speed as a Feature (for the House)

The auctioneer speaks extremely fast — bids fly by in seconds. This is deliberate:
- Speed creates **urgency and FOMO**
- Buyers have no time to second-guess
- Prices get pushed up in the heat of the moment

**Tradeoff for us:** Async bidding with countdown timers gives buyers time to think. This likely means **lower final prices** (good for buyers, but potentially harder to sell to current auction sellers). We need to frame this honestly: our sellers may get slightly lower prices but keep more after fees.

---

## 5. The Wednesday Crowd: Who Actually Attends?

The auction was packed on a Wednesday — a working day. The crowd is primarily:
- **Professional dealers and resellers** (this is their full-time job)
- **Commercial farmers** restocking herds
- **Agents** buying on behalf of absent clients

**Who's NOT there:**
- Employed people who can't take a Wednesday off
- Rural smallholders outside the geographic catchment
- Diaspora Zimbabweans buying for family back home
- Anyone priced out by the US$1,000 deposit

**Our real market:** The people who *want* cattle but *can't attend*. This is our wedge — geographic and schedule accessibility.

---

## 6. Logistics: Transport as a Pain Point

Neither the auction house nor the buyer/seller ecosystem has a clean transport solution. After winning a bid, the buyer must independently arrange:
- A truck or trailer
- Fuel
- Loading/offloading labour
- Police clearance for moving livestock

**Our opportunity:** Don't build a logistics company. Instead:
- Integrate a **verified transporter directory** into the platform
- Let buyers book transport at checkout
- Take a small referral fee
- This solves the "how do I get cattle from Masvingo to Harare" problem and expands our buyer pool geographically

---

## 7. Police Clearance: Non-Negotiable Legal Requirement

A policewoman is stationed at the auction to issue clearance for every animal sold. This is a ZRP requirement to prevent cattle theft — you cannot legally move livestock without it.

**We cannot skip this.** But we can digitize it:
- Start with **photo upload** of the physical clearance certificate
- Long-term: work with ZRP to issue digital clearance certificates linked to platform listings
- This becomes a trust feature, not a burden ("Every animal on ZimLivestock has verified police clearance")

---

## 8. The Face-to-Face Problem

The entire auction experience is built on physical presence:
- Buyers inspect animals in person (teeth, hooves, udders, temperament)
- The auctioneer reads the room
- Social trust — regulars know each other
- Disputes are resolved on the spot

**How we compensate digitally:**
- **Video listings** (not just photos) — 360-degree walk-around, close-ups
- **Seller verification** with ratings and history
- **Escrow payments** — money doesn't release until buyer confirms receipt
- **Dispute resolution** process built into the platform
- **Veterinary certificates** as optional listing attachments

## Strategic Summary

### What the Auction House Sells
1. **Urgency** — fast-paced bidding drives prices up
2. **Trust** — deposits, police clearance, physical inspection
3. **Liquidity** — many buyers in one room at one time

### How ZimLivestock Matches Each

| Auction House | ZimLivestock |
|---------------|-------------|
| Speed/urgency | Timed auctions with push notifications |
| US$1,000 deposit | US$50-100 refundable escrow via Paynow |
| Physical inspection | Video listings + vet certs + seller ratings |
| Police clearance | Digital clearance upload/verification |
| 12% fees | 5-6% fees |
| Wednesday-only, one location | 24/7, nationwide, anyone with a phone |
| No transport help | Integrated transporter directory |
| Face-to-face disputes | In-app dispute resolution + escrow |

### Our Wedge: The Buyers Who Can't Attend
- Employed professionals
- Rural smallholders outside catchment
- Diaspora buyers
- Anyone priced out by the US$1,000 deposit

### Real Numbers: Active Bidders vs. Spectators

The highest bid card observed was **45** — meaning the maximum number of active bidders in the room was around 30-50. The rest of the crowd (easily 150+) are spectators: sellers scoping prices, first-timers learning the process, and people there for the social event.

**What this means for us:**
- We don't need 200 users to match an auction house. We need **~45 serious bidders** per auction. That's achievable.
- Spectators still matter — they create the *feeling* of competition. A buyer bids more aggressively when 200 people are watching. We lose that psychological pressure on a screen.
- **Product implication:** "X people watching this auction" badges and real-time bid counts can partially recreate crowd energy digitally.

### Biggest Risk
**Liquidity.** But the bar is lower than it looks. We don't need to fill a room — we need ~45 active bidders per listing. Transport partnerships expand geographic reach (more potential buyers per listing), and lower fees attract price-sensitive sellers.
